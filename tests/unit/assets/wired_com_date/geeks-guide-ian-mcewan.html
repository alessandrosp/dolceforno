Ian McEwan Doesn’t Hate Science Fiction

In a recent interview British novelist Ian McEwan seemed to suggest that science fiction is only about “traveling at 10 times the speed of light in antigravity boots,” in contrast to his own novel Machines Like Me, which he says explores the “human dilemmas” involved with artificial intelligence. Science fiction fans bristled, questioning whether McEwan had ever actually read any science fiction, but McEwan now insists that he’s been misunderstood.

“I was a little taken aback at how some rather offhand remarks of mine should cause such a storm,” McEwan says in Episode 359 of the Geek’s Guide to the Galaxy podcast. “And actually I’ve read a fair amount of science fiction over a lifetime.”

Among the science fiction authors that he’s read and enjoyed are Philip K. Dick, Walter M. Miller, Brian Aldiss, Ursula K. Le Guin, Frank Herbert, Stanislaw Lem, Tom Disch, and Olaf Stapledon. McEwan is also a great fan of the movie Blade Runner, which is referenced in Machines Like Me.

“I actually put a nod towards Blade Runner in Adam’s final speeches, after he’s been attacked by Charlie,” McEwan says. “There’s a very self-conscious nod to that famous farewell in the rain.”

And while science fiction works like Blade Runner are a definite influence on Machines Like Me, McEwan notes that there are many other influences as well. “I’d be very happy for my novel to be called science fiction, but it’s also a counterfactual novel, it’s also a historical novel, it’s also a moral dilemma novel, in a well-established traditional form within the literary novel,” he says. “I’m very happy if they want to call my novel science fiction, even honored. But it’s much else, that’s all I’m trying to say.”

Ultimately he feels that literary categories are increasingly unhelpful and shouldn’t be taken too seriously.

“Science fiction writers and thriller writers and traditional so-called ‘literary’ novelists are all novelists,” he says, “and they finally have to be judged on how good they are, not on which category they belong in.”

Listen to the complete interview with McEwan in Episode 359 of Geek’s Guide to the Galaxy (above). And check out some highlights from the discussion below.

Ian McEwan on new technology:

“We get used to [new technology] with astonishing rapidity. I remember being in Manhattan some years ago and seeing a huge line down the street—maybe 400 yards, around the block—and I asked my friend what was going on. I thought maybe Van Morrison was in town or something. But no, they were lining up for an iPhone 4 or 5. You ask yourself now, where are all those iPhone 4s and 5s? They’re at the bottom of your sock drawer, or they’re in the hands of your grandchildren, who are playing games on them. They’ve just become yesterday’s junk. … We’re like children on Christmas Day opening presents. They open one and barely absorb it before there’s the excitement of opening another. So I think we’re rather like children in that respect.”

Ian McEwan on autonomous vehicles:

“We should rightly feel uneasy, that even though we’re pretty clumsy in [emergencies], handing over that responsibility is a big step. … [What happened with the Boeing 737 MAX] rather reminded me of the last moments of 2001: A Space Odyssey, talking to HAL, when HAL has made his decision. So I think we will really need to be having conversations about moral decision-taking and what we hand over.”

Ian McEwan on biology:

“I decided—having been asked if I’d write the follow-up to The Fly—to try to base it as closely as possible on contemporary knowledge. I found a book called How to Make a Fly—although that fly was not a housefly, as in the movie, it was a fruit fly. But I tried to build [the script] around, as much as possible, a proper understanding of genetics. That was in the mid-’90s, and how quickly what I knew fell out of date. I remember my son William went to study biology at the University College in London, and he showed me [a note] right at the beginning of his course that said, ‘Please don’t read anything about genetics before 1997.’ Now, we were in the year 2001, so the speed with which that subject was changing was astonishing.”

Ian McEwan on literary categories:

“I think that the novel—and I think we should usually talk about ‘the novel’ rather than ‘the literary novel’ or ‘the science fiction novel’—but the novel is a very good means of examining colossal social change, but also of [examining] the moral dilemmas that new technologies are going to make us confront. I think there could be a resurgence, a revitalization, of the form, in which—quite possibly—concepts and categories of ‘literary’ novels up against ‘science fiction’ novels will completely vanish, because we’ll need the technical grasp of technologies that the best science fiction yields to us, and we’ll need the traditional examinations of moral dilemmas that the literary novel has always prided itself upon. So I look forward to these categories just dissolving.”
